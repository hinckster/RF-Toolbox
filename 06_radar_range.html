Radar Range Equation

Full Skolnik radar range equation — R vs RCS curve, Pr vs range, clutter comparison, pulse compression gain.

Radar Range Equation (Skolnik 2.1)
R⁴ = Pt·Gt·Gr·λ²·σ / [(4π)³·L·S_min]
S_min = kTB·NF·(S/N)_min / T_i
In dB: R_max = ¼ · [Pt+Gt+Gr+2·10log(λ)+σ_dBsm −(4π)³_dB−L−S_min]
λ = c/f. Each term varies as R⁴ — the "fourth-power law".
Received Power
Pr = Pt·Gt·Gr·λ²·σ / [(4π)³·R⁴·L]
= EIRP + Gr + 10log(σ) − 30log(R) + 20log(λ) − const
Falls as R⁻⁴ (40 dB/decade) — every 2× range = −12 dB received power.
Pulse Compression
G_pc = BW·τ = TBP
Range_compressed = Range_uncompressed · TBP^(1/4)
R_max ∝ (Pt·τ·BW)^(1/4) = (E_total)^(1/4)
Time-bandwidth product. LFM chirp: BW = Δf, τ = pulse width.

Radar Range Equation — Help
What this tool calculates
Solves the Skolnik radar range equation R⁴ = P_t·G_t·G_r·λ²·σ / [(4π)³·L·S_min] to find the maximum detection range for a monostatic radar. It also computes received power Pr as a function of range (the R⁻⁴ fourth-power law) and shows how linear frequency-modulated (LFM) pulse compression increases effective range by the time-bandwidth product factor TBP^(1/4).
Sliders & inputs
  • Tx Power P_t — Peak transmitted power in watts (1 W to 10 kW). Click the unit label to toggle between W and kW. Scales as R_max ∝ P_t^(1/4).
  • Antenna Gain G_t = G_r — Monostatic assumption: transmit and receive antenna gain are equal, 0–50 dBi. A large dish at 40 dBi is typical for surveillance radar.
  • Frequency f — Operating frequency 0.1–100 GHz. Affects wavelength λ = c/f, which enters the equation as λ². Toggle label between GHz and MHz.
  • Target RCS σ — Radar cross section in dBsm, −30 to +40 dBsm. A human is roughly −10 dBsm; a large ship is +35 dBsm.
  • System Loss L — Aggregate one-way RF losses (plumbing, radome, atmospheric), 0–20 dB. Typical operational systems carry 3–8 dB.
  • S_min — Minimum detectable signal at the receiver, −130 to −60 dBm. Set by noise figure, bandwidth, and required SNR (S_min = kTB·NF·SNR_min).
  • Pulse width τ — LFM chirp pulse duration, 10 ns–100 µs. Longer pulses carry more energy but degrade range resolution.
  • Chirp BW — LFM swept bandwidth, 0.1 MHz–1 GHz. Pulse compression gain = BW × τ (time-bandwidth product).
How to read the charts
Top chart — Max Range vs Target RCS: X-axis is RCS (dBsm), Y-axis is detection range (km). The blue solid line is uncompressed range; the green dashed line is pulse-compressed range. The red dot marks the currently selected RCS. Clutter reference markers show where common targets fall.

Bottom chart — Received Power vs Range: X-axis is range (km, log scale), Y-axis is received power (dBm). The curve falls at 40 dB/decade (R⁻⁴). The horizontal dashed orange line is S_min — where the curve crosses S_min is R_max. Vertical colored tick marks show reference clutter RCS levels at a fixed range for comparison.
Key results
  • Max Range — Uncompressed R_max in km at the selected RCS and system parameters.
  • λ — Wavelength in centimetres, computed from frequency. Determines aperture efficiency and RCS scaling.
  • Pulse Comp. Gain — 10·log₁₀(BW·τ) in dB. The SNR improvement from pulse compression; also the range-sidelobe suppression requirement.
  • R_max (compressed) — Effective detection range after pulse compression: R_uncompressed × TBP^(1/4).
  • EIRP — Equivalent isotropically radiated power (P_t + G_t) in dBm. The transmit figure of merit.
  • Pr @ 10 km — Received power from the selected RCS target at exactly 10 km, useful as a sanity-check reference level.
Things to try
  • Load the Automotive 77 GHz FMCW preset, then increase chirp BW to 500 MHz — watch how pulse compression alone extends range without increasing transmit power.
  • Set RCS to −30 dBsm (insect-size target) and observe how range collapses compared to an airliner at +20 dBsm — a 50 dBsm difference yields only 10× range change because of the fourth-power law.
  • With the Weather Radar preset loaded, reduce S_min from −110 to −120 dBm to simulate a lower-noise receiver — note that every 6 dB improvement in S_min yields only ~1.6× range improvement (fourth-root law).
Reference
Skolnik, M. I. (2001). Introduction to Radar Systems (3rd ed.), Chapters 2–3. McGraw-Hill. Equation 2-1 (radar range equation) and Section 11.5 (pulse compression).
